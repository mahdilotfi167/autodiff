# 28. Synthetic Data, Self-Play & the Data Frontier

## Motivation

We're running out of high-quality human-generated text. The internet contains roughly 5-10 trillion tokens of unique quality text. Training frontier models already consumes a significant fraction of this. The next generation of models needs either better data efficiency or new sources of data. This lecture covers the rapidly evolving landscape of synthetic data generation, its promises, its pitfalls, and the techniques that actually work.

---

## 28.1 The Data Wall

### The arithmetic of data scarcity

Chinchilla-optimal training: $D^* \approx 20N$.

| Model size | Tokens needed | Status |
|---|---|---|
| 7B | 140B | Easy — plenty of data |
| 70B | 1.4T | Feasible — requires careful curation |
| 400B | 8T | Tight — approaching total available quality data |
| 1T+ | 20T+ | **Exceeds estimated high-quality internet text** |

The world produces ~5-10T tokens of unique, high-quality text per year (books, papers, quality web pages). But most internet text is low quality (spam, boilerplate, duplicates). After filtering, the usable pool is finite and nearly exhausted.

### Why "just repeat the data" doesn't work

Muennighoff et al. (2023): Training on repeated data shows diminishing returns:

- 1-4 repetitions: Nearly equivalent to unique data
- 4-16 repetitions: Each repetition worth ~50-70% of a unique token
- 16+ repetitions: Marginal value drops sharply; model memorizes rather than generalizes

$$\text{Effective tokens} \approx D_{\text{unique}} \times \left(1 + \frac{R - 1}{1 + \alpha R}\right)$$

where $R$ is the number of repetitions and $\alpha$ controls the diminishing returns. For $R = 100$, effective tokens might be only $3\text{-}5 \times D_{\text{unique}}$, not $100\times$.

## 28.2 Synthetic Data: The Promise

### What is synthetic data?

Data generated by a model rather than a human. Types:

| Type | Method | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Distillation** | Large model generates, small model trains | GPT-4 generates training data for GPT-3.5 |
| **Self-improvement** | Model generates data for itself | Same model generates solutions, trains on correct ones |
| **Augmentation** | Transform existing examples | Rephrase, translate, add noise |
| **Environment-generated** | Model interacts with an environment | Code execution feedback, math verification |
| **Self-play** | Two model instances compete/collaborate | Debate, adversarial testing |

### Why it can work

1. **Verification is cheaper than generation.** It's hard to write a correct proof, but easy to check one. A model can generate 1000 proofs, verify with a checker, and keep the correct ones.

2. **Coverage amplification.** A model that's correct 30% of the time can generate 100 attempts → ~100% chance of at least one correct answer → train on the correct one.

3. **Task diversity at scale.** Human labelers produce ~100 examples/day. A model produces ~100,000 examples/day. Even if quality is lower, the diversity can be higher.

## 28.3 Model Collapse: When Synthetic Data Destroys

### The phenomenon

Shumailov et al. (2023): When models are trained on data generated by previous models (iteratively), quality degrades catastrophically:

$$M_0 \xrightarrow{\text{generates}} D_1 \xrightarrow{\text{trains}} M_1 \xrightarrow{\text{generates}} D_2 \xrightarrow{\text{trains}} M_2 \xrightarrow{\text{generates}} \cdots$$

After a few generations, the model produces low-quality, repetitive, degenerate output. The distribution "collapses" to a narrow mode.

### Why it happens (the mathematical argument)

Each model generation introduces a small approximation error. If the true data distribution is $P$ and model $M_t$ approximates $P_t$:

$$P_{t+1} = M_t(P_t) \approx P_t + \epsilon_t$$

The errors accumulate: $P_n \approx P + \sum_{t=0}^{n} \epsilon_t$

But it's worse than simple accumulation. Each model:
1. **Overestimates high-probability modes** (frequently sampled → well-represented in synthetic data)
2. **Underestimates low-probability tails** (rarely sampled → missing from synthetic data)
3. The next model trains on this biased data → amplifies the bias

Over iterations: the distribution narrows around the modes, the tails vanish, and diversity collapses.

### The "minority erasure" problem

Tails of the distribution (rare topics, unusual writing styles, minority perspectives) are undersampled in synthetic data → the next model doesn't learn them → they're even less likely in the next generation → eventually they're completely absent.

This is not just a quality issue — it's an equity issue. Synthetic data amplifies majority patterns and erases minorities.

### Prevention strategies

| Strategy | Mechanism | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Always mix real data** | Anchor the distribution to reality | Requires maintaining real data supply |
| **Nucleus/entropy filtering** | Remove low-diversity synthetic examples | Moderate compute |
| **Freshness constraint** | Only one generation of synthetic data | Limits the loop |
| **Verification** | Only keep synthetic data that passes checks | Need automated verifiers |
| **Distribution matching** | Ensure synthetic data statistics match real data | Complex, but effective |

## 28.4 Distillation: Teaching Small Models from Large Ones

### Knowledge distillation fundamentals

Given a teacher model $T$ and a student model $S$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{distill}} = (1-\alpha) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}(S(x), y) + \alpha \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(T_\tau(x) \| S_\tau(x))$$

where $T_\tau$ and $S_\tau$ are the teacher and student outputs at temperature $\tau$.

**Why temperature matters**: At $\tau > 1$, the teacher's soft labels reveal the relative probabilities of incorrect answers:
- Teacher: "Paris 0.9, Lyon 0.05, Marseille 0.03, London 0.02"
- Hard label: "Paris 1.0, everything else 0.0"

The soft labels carry **more information per example** — the student learns that Lyon is more plausible than London, which hard labels don't convey.

### Distillation for LLMs (practical approaches)

**1. Response distillation** (most common):
- Teacher generates high-quality responses to diverse prompts
- Student fine-tunes on (prompt, teacher_response) pairs
- Simple, scalable, but loses the teacher's uncertainty information

**2. Logit distillation** (when you have access to teacher logits):
- For each token position, match the student's output distribution to the teacher's
- Much more information per example (entire vocabulary distribution, not just one token)
- Requires teacher inference at every training step → expensive

**3. On-policy distillation**:
- Student generates; teacher scores; student trains on high-scored outputs
- The student generates from its own distribution → no distribution mismatch
- More robust but requires iterative training

### The "impossible distillation" result

Gudibande et al. (2023): Distilling from GPT-4 to a small model can match GPT-4's **style** (formatting, tone, fluency) but NOT its **knowledge** (factual accuracy, reasoning depth).

**Why**: Style is a simple pattern that transfers easily. Knowledge requires capacity (parameters) that the small student doesn't have. You can teach a small model to *sound* smart but not to *be* smart.

**Implication**: Distillation is not a substitute for pretraining. It's a way to transfer behavioral patterns, not deep capabilities.

## 28.5 Self-Improvement & Iterative Refinement

### The STaR approach (Self-Taught Reasoner)

Zelikman et al. (2022):

1. Model attempts math problems with chain-of-thought
2. Keep only the (question, reasoning, correct_answer) triples
3. Fine-tune the model on these successful attempts
4. Repeat from step 1

Each iteration, the model gets slightly better at reasoning → generates more correct examples → trains on better data → improves further.

**The bootstrapping curve**: Performance improves logarithmically with iterations, eventually plateauing when the model can't generate correct answers for the remaining hard problems.

### Rejection sampling fine-tuning (RFT)

A simpler variant used widely in practice:

1. For each training prompt, generate $N$ responses (e.g., $N=64$)
2. Use a verifier (test suite for code, automatic checker for math) to identify correct responses
3. Fine-tune on the correct responses

This is essentially **best-of-N as training data**. The key insight: you're training the model on examples from its own distribution that happen to be correct, shifting the distribution toward correctness.

```python
# Rejection sampling fine-tuning
correct_examples = []
for problem in problems:
    responses = []
    for _ in range(N):
        response = model.generate(problem, temperature=0.7)
        if verifier(problem, response):  # Automatic check
            responses.append(response)
    if responses:
        correct_examples.append((problem, random.choice(responses)))

# Fine-tune on correct examples
model.finetune(correct_examples)
```

### When self-improvement works

Self-improvement requires a **verification signal** — some way to tell correct from incorrect:

| Domain | Verification signal | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Code | Unit tests, execution | High (automated, reliable) |
| Math | Symbolic verification, answer check | High for final answer, medium for reasoning |
| Logic | Formal proof checkers | Very high |
| Creative writing | None (subjective) | Cannot self-improve without human/LLM judge |
| Factual Q&A | Retrieval verification | Medium |

**The key constraint**: Without a reliable verifier, self-improvement is self-reinforcing noise. The model amplifies its own mistakes.

## 28.6 Self-Play: Competition Breeds Quality

### The idea

Two instances of the same model compete or debate, generating training data from the interaction.

### Debate as alignment

Irving et al. (2018): Two models argue opposing positions. A human judge picks the more convincing argument. Training signal: "make arguments the judge finds compelling."

```
Model A: "X is true because [evidence]"
Model B: "X is false because [counter-evidence]"
Judge: A wins (B's evidence was weaker)
Training signal: reward A-style arguments, penalize B-style
```

**Why this helps alignment**: A deceptive argument is vulnerable to a competent opponent who points out flaws. Through training with debate, models learn to make **genuinely correct** (not just persuasive) arguments.

### Constitutional AI's self-play component

Anthropic's Constitutional AI uses a form of self-play:

1. Model generates a response (potentially harmful)
2. Model critiques its own response using constitutional principles
3. Model revises the response based on its critique
4. Train on (prompt, revised_response) pairs

The model is "playing both roles" — generator and critic. The constitution provides the rules of the "game."

### AlphaProof-style mathematical self-play

DeepMind's approach to mathematical reasoning:

1. Model proposes a formal proof step
2. Proof assistant (Lean, Isabelle) verifies correctness
3. If correct: continue. If incorrect: backtrack and try another step
4. Training signal: proof steps that led to completed proofs

This is MCTS (Monte Carlo Tree Search) applied to theorem proving: the model is playing against the mathematical truth. No human labels needed — the verifier is the environment.

## 28.7 Data Valuation: Not All Data Is Equal

### Which examples matter?

Different training examples contribute differently to model quality. **Data valuation** assigns a score to each example based on its impact on model performance.

### Influence functions

The influence of training example $z_i$ on test loss is approximately:

$$\text{Influence}(z_i) = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(z_{\text{test}})^T \mathbf{H}^{-1} \nabla_\theta \ell(z_i)$$

where $\mathbf{H}$ is the Hessian. This measures how much removing $z_i$ from training would change the test loss.

**Impractical for LLMs** (Hessian is $N \times N$ where $N \sim 10^{10}$), but the concept is important:
- Some examples improve the model a lot (high influence)
- Some examples are neutral
- Some examples actively hurt (harmful influence — noisy, mislabeled, or adversarial data)

### Practical approximations

**1. Perplexity-based selection**: Keep examples with moderate perplexity. Too low PPL → boring/repetitive. Too high PPL → noise/gibberish.

**2. DSIR (Data Selection via Importance Reweighting)**: Score training examples by similarity to a target distribution:
$$w(x) = \frac{P_{\text{target}}(x)}{P_{\text{training}}(x)}$$
Upweight examples that look like your target domain, downweight others.

**3. Deduplication as data quality**: Removing duplicates doesn't just save compute — it improves quality by preventing memorization of specific phrasings.

### The data quality hierarchy

In practice, data quality follows roughly this hierarchy:

$$\text{Human expert} > \text{Human amateur} > \text{Curated web} > \text{Filtered web} > \text{Raw web} > \text{Synthetic}$$

But the volume relationship is inverted:

$$\text{Synthetic} > \text{Raw web} > \text{Filtered web} > \text{Curated} > \text{Human}$$

The art is finding the right mix.

## 28.8 The Current Frontier

### What works now (2024-2026):

1. **Synthetic data for reasoning** (math, code): Verified synthetic data from STaR, RFT, and AlphaProof-style methods consistently improves reasoning capabilities. The verifier is the bottleneck.

2. **Distillation for style** (instruction following, formatting): Small models distilled from large ones can match behavioral quality on many tasks.

3. **Data augmentation** (rephrasing, translation): Expanding existing high-quality data through paraphrasing and multilingual translation creates useful diversity.

4. **Domain-specific synthetic** (medical, legal, scientific): Large models generate domain text, experts validate a fraction, models train on validated subset. Cost-effective for niche domains.

### What doesn't work (yet):

1. **Iterative self-generation without verification**: Model collapse is the norm, not the exception.

2. **Synthetic data for factual knowledge**: Models hallucinate facts → synthetic data propagates hallucinations.

3. **Full replacement of human data**: Every successful synthetic data method still requires a large base of real data.

### The open question

Can synthetic data generation + verification create an **unbounded** supply of useful training data? Or is there a fundamental limit to self-improvement without new external information?

The optimistic view (self-play can discover new knowledge): Mathematics and formal reasoning are closed systems — a model can discover new proofs that no human has written.

The pessimistic view (garbage in, garbage out): Without external grounding, models can only recombine what they've already seen, eventually reaching a creative ceiling.

The truth is likely domain-dependent: self-play works for formal, verifiable domains (math, code, logic); it fails for open-ended, real-world knowledge that requires external observation.
